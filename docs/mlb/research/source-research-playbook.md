# The complete playbook for systematic MLB betting models

**Building a profitable MLB betting model requires a pitcher-centric architecture, weather-aware totals pricing, and ruthless attention to closing line value—not win rate.** The MLB moneyline market is moderately efficient but significantly more beatable than NFL spreads, particularly in derivative markets like first-five-innings lines, totals, and strikeout props. For an experienced NCAAB modeler extending to baseball, the critical infrastructure shifts are: replacing spread-based pricing with win-probability-to-moneyline conversion, adding pitcher-specific projections as the primary model driver (no equivalent exists in basketball), and building a daily automation pipeline capable of processing 15 games per day with lineup-confirmed, weather-adjusted predictions. The academic literature confirms MLB markets exhibit small but real inefficiencies—Robbins (2023) found statistically significant pricing gaps across 155,000+ contests—while the most robust backtesting shows realistic ROI of **2–5% annually** for well-calibrated models, achievable even at sub-50% win rates when leaning into plus-money underdogs.

## The voices worth listening to in MLB betting analytics

The MLB betting modeling space has a surprisingly small number of truly authoritative voices. **Ed Miller and Matthew Davidow** are the essential authors: *The Logic of Sports Betting* (2019) covers market structure fundamentals, while *Interception* (2023) exposes modern sportsbook weaknesses in props, SGPs, and in-game markets. Davidow has beaten major sports betting markets for 15+ years and co-founded two private analytics firms. Their core insight—that sportsbooks are "sophisticated technology products held together with duct tape and crazy glue under the hood"—frames the entire opportunity.

**Joe Peta's** *Trading Bases* (2013) remains the MLB-specific betting bible, applying Wall Street modeling to baseball: team winning percentages → WAR-based pitcher adjustments → bullpen/lineup adjustments → home-field → win probability → compare to market odds. This framework has been reimplemented in Python by multiple developers and extended with deep learning approaches. On the analytics side, **Tom Tango** (Senior Data Architect at MLB, @tangotiger) pioneered the sabermetric foundations that feed every modern model, and his co-authored *The Book: Playing the Percentages in Baseball* remains essential for understanding platoon splits, lineup optimization, and bullpen leverage.

For ongoing education, **Circles Off** (hosted by Rob Pizzola on The Hammer Betting Network) is the premier podcast—deep interviews with professional bettors and trading desk operators. **Gill Alexander** on VSiN brings decades of MLB handicapping expertise. The platform **Unabated** (founded by Captain Jack Andrews, 20+ year professional gambler) provides the sharpest tools for serious MLB bettors: their proprietary "Unabated Line" creates a vig-free consensus weighted by how quickly each book reaches closing numbers for baseball specifically. **Betstamp** offers essential CLV tracking. On the projection side, **Derek Carty's THE BAT X** bridges DFS projections and sports betting through EV Analytics, while **Dan Szymborski's ZiPS** and the **Steamer** system provide freely accessible projections on FanGraphs.

The academic literature is worth knowing. Woodland and Woodland (1994) found a **reverse favorite-longshot bias** in MLB—bettors overbet favorites, unlike horse racing. Hubáček, Šourek, and Železný (2019) demonstrated that decorrelating your model from the bookmaker's model is critical: if your model agrees with the book, you lose to vig. Perhaps most importantly, a 2023 arXiv paper (2303.06021) showed that **model calibration is more important than accuracy**—calibration-based model selection produced +34.69% ROI versus -35.17% for accuracy-based selection. The FiveThirtyEight MLB Elo model, now archived on GitHub with game-by-game ratings back to 1871, provides a fully documented, open-source reference implementation.

## Starting pitchers are the single most important model input

No other major sport has one player dominate a betting line the way starting pitchers dominate MLB. FiveThirtyEight found that adding pitcher adjustments improves game-prediction accuracy by approximately one full percentage point—an enormous edge in a market where 2–3% total ROI is excellent. At his peak, Pedro Martínez was worth ~109 Elo points, equivalent to a **15 percentage-point boost** to Boston's win probability per start.

The most predictive pitching metrics for betting purposes follow a clear hierarchy. **Stuff+** is the most valuable early-season metric because it stabilizes after just 80 pitches—before a starter has completed two outings. Pitching+ (combining Stuff+ and Location+) outpredicts all current projection systems for relievers before the season begins. After Stuff+, **xFIP and SIERA** are the workhorses, each achieving approximately r ≈ 0.52 for predicting next-year ERA—substantially better than ERA itself. **K-BB%** is the single most telling rate stat for pitcher effectiveness; K% stabilizes at ~150 batters faced, making it usable by mid-April. CSW% (Called Strike + Whiff percentage) captures both command and stuff quality. FIP, while noisier than xFIP, strips defense and luck effectively.

The critical modeling principle is exponential decay weighting: recent starts weighted more heavily than season-long or career stats. FiveThirtyEight uses rolling Game Scores normalized for era, stadium, and opponent. For preseason, composite projections consistently outperform any single system—blend ZiPS, Steamer, PECOTA, and THE BAT at roughly equal weights, then phase in current-season data gradually: ~90% projections in April → ~50/50 by mid-May → ~70–80% current season by July.

A common trap is batter-versus-pitcher history. The consensus from FanGraphs and *The Book* is clear: **BvP stats are mostly noise**. You need 50+ plate appearances for minimal signal, and even then the matchup occurred over years during which both players changed. Use BvP only as a minor tiebreaker for strikeout props with extreme sample sizes.

### Bullpen modeling requires availability tracking above all

The standard architecture splits pitcher impact approximately **two-thirds starter, one-third bullpen**, adjusted dynamically based on expected starter innings. Bullpen ERA is ~1 full run more variable than starter ERA due to defense and luck, making xFIP and SIERA essential over raw ERA. The highest-signal bullpen metrics are hard-contact avoidance (barrel rate, hard-hit%), ground-ball rate, and LOB% (strand rate, r = -0.818 with bullpen ERA)—but LOB% is largely outside pitcher control, so regress aggressively.

The real edge in bullpen modeling is **availability tracking**: back-to-back appearances degrade performance measurably, and tracking innings pitched in the last 3 and 7 days, pitches thrown in the most recent outing, and days since the closer's last appearance creates information asymmetry versus books that use cruder approximations. Opener detection is increasingly important: FiveThirtyEight flags openers when a pitcher has 5+ career appearances, hasn't exceeded 2 IP in the last 10 outings, and has opened at least once in the last 20 appearances. When an opener is detected, the model should default to team-average pitcher adjustment.

### Park factors and weather create the largest totals edges

Park effects in MLB are enormous compared to any venue effect in basketball or football. **Coors Field's** thin air causes balls to travel ~9% farther than sea-level parks and reduces breaking-pitch movement by ~3 inches. Dodger Stadium leads MLB with a 132 HR factor. But the modeling insight is subtler: parks affect singles, doubles, triples, strikeouts, and walks independently—not just home runs. Apply event-specific park factors, not a single run-scoring multiplier, and account for handedness interaction (Yankee Stadium's short right-field porch disproportionately benefits left-handed batters).

**Weather is the most consistently profitable simple variable for totals betting.** Wind blowing in from center field at 5+ mph produced unders at a **55.5% rate** (764-613-76) since 2005, with only one losing season in 14 years. Wind blowing out correlates with overs but less consistently. At Wrigley Field specifically, a 10 mph change in wind speed shifts average HR distance by ~61 feet. Temperature effects are real but modest—a ball hit at 45°F travels only ~20 feet shorter than at 95°F. Humidity is counterintuitive: humid air is actually less dense (water vapor is lighter than nitrogen/oxygen), but it makes the ball itself heavier and less elastic, slightly reducing distance.

Umpire assignments can shift true game totals by **0.3–0.7 runs**, but oddsmakers are aware of this. The edge is not in blindly following umpire stats—it's in finding spots where umpire tendencies interact with specific pitcher styles, weather, and park factors in non-obvious ways. Command pitchers who paint corners benefit massively from wide strike zones; power pitchers are less affected.

Platoon splits are among the most meaningful and stable effects in baseball. The average platoon gap can reach 60+ wRC+ points in extreme cases (Joc Pederson: 128 wRC+ vs. RHP, 59 vs. LHP). Model lineup handedness composition against starting pitcher handedness as a primary input. Rest and travel effects are real but small: Northwestern University's study of 20 years of MLB data found **eastward travel is significantly worse** than westward, consistent with circadian rhythm research, and jet lag can cancel out home-field advantage entirely.

## The data infrastructure: pybaseball is the foundation

For a Python-based system, **pybaseball** (`pip install pybaseball`) is the essential library, unifying access to Baseball Savant (Statcast), FanGraphs, and Baseball Reference into pandas DataFrames. Key functions include `statcast(start_dt, end_dt)` for pitch-level data (~700,000+ pitches per season with exit velocity, spin rate, launch angle, and expected stats), `pitching_stats()` and `batting_stats()` for FanGraphs leaderboards with 334 columns including WAR, FIP, xERA, CSW%, and barrel%, and `playerid_lookup()` for cross-referencing player IDs across systems. Enable caching with `pybaseball.cache.enable()` for large Statcast pulls.

The **MLB Stats API** (`pip install MLB-StatsAPI`) provides free, unauthenticated access to schedules with probable pitchers, live game feeds with play-by-play and lineups, rosters, standings, and injury reports. Base URL: `https://statsapi.mlb.com/api/v1/`. This API drives your daily pipeline for schedule ingestion and lineup confirmation.

For odds data, **The Odds API** is the best option for live and semi-historical data, covering 40+ sportsbooks including Pinnacle, DraftKings, and FanDuel across moneyline, run line, totals, and player props. The free tier offers 500 calls/month for development; the $49/month tier provides 90,000 calls for production use. For backtesting, **SportsBookReview Online** archives provide free historical opening/closing lines (no longer updated but archives remain), **sports-statistics.com** offers free 2010–2021 data, and **Odds Warehouse** sells comprehensive multi-season datasets for $39–79. FiveThirtyEight's archived Elo CSV on GitHub includes game-by-game ratings and probabilities back to 1871.

Weather data comes from **OpenWeatherMap** (free tier: 1,000 calls/day) queried with stadium latitude/longitude coordinates, while **Ballpark Pal** provides game-specific weather-adjusted park factors via a premium subscription. For lineups, **RotoWire's daily lineups page** is the industry standard, typically publishing confirmed lineups 2–4 hours before first pitch. The MLB Stats API's live game feed also includes confirmed lineups once officially posted.

### Database schema and daily pipeline design

Extending an existing SQLite/SQLAlchemy sports betting system requires these MLB-specific tables: `pitcher_game_logs` (IP, K, BB, HR, velocity, spin rate, CSW%, barrel%, pitch mix), `batter_game_logs` (PA, exit velocity, launch angle, xwOBA), `lineups` (game_pk, batting order, player_id, confirmed timestamp), `weather` (temperature, wind speed/direction, humidity, dome flag), `park_factors` (event-specific: HR, runs, hits, strikeouts by handedness), `umpires` (career K%, BB%, runs per game), and `odds_history` (timestamped snapshots for line movement analysis). Use MLB's `game_pk` as the universal game identifier, and store cross-reference player IDs (MLBAM, FanGraphs, BBRef, Retrosheet) in a players table.

The daily pipeline runs on four cycles. Morning (8–9 AM ET): pull schedule, probable pitchers, weather forecasts, opening odds, and injuries. Midday (11 AM–1 PM): monitor lineup confirmations every 30 minutes via RotoWire scraping, update rolling pitcher/batter stats, re-pull odds. Pre-game (2–4 PM): run prediction model, compare to market, apply Kelly sizing, log recommendations. Post-game (11 PM): pull results and Statcast data via pybaseball, update game logs, recompute rolling statistics, record bet results with CLV.

Several open-source GitHub repositories provide useful reference architectures. **stormlightlabs/baseball** offers a complete ETL pipeline loading Lahman, Retrosheet, FanGraphs, and weather data with materialized views. **brendanahart/MLBDailyProjections** implements a full daily projection system with MySQL and ZiPS integration. The FiveThirtyEight NFL Elo codebase includes an `eval.py` framework directly adaptable to MLB backtesting.

## Build simple first, validate obsessively, add complexity incrementally

The recommended model-building progression starts with a **team-level Elo or Pythagorean system** as the foundation. FiveThirtyEight's Elo uses 24 points for home-field advantage, travel penalties up to ~4 points (calculated as miles_traveled^(1/3) × -0.31), and 2.3 points per rest day. Pythagorean expectation with an exponent of **1.83** (not 2, per Jay Huemann's backtesting) provides a strong baseline. Layer starting pitcher adjustments second—this single addition improves prediction accuracy more than any other variable. Then add park factors, confirmed lineup data, bullpen strength, weather, and umpire tendencies in that order. Each addition should demonstrably improve out-of-sample performance before proceeding to the next.

The most dangerous pitfall is **look-ahead bias**: never use data unavailable at prediction time. This includes full-season winning percentages to predict individual games within that season, or lineup data before it's confirmed. Walk-forward validation for MLB's continuous 162-game season differs fundamentally from NCAAB's discrete season structure. Use rolling windows: train through April → validate on May → retrain through May → validate on June. Start with ~90% projection system weight in early April and gradually phase in observed data as metrics stabilize.

Understanding stabilization rates is critical for avoiding sample-size traps. Swing% stabilizes at ~50 plate appearances, strikeout rate at ~150 PA, walk rate at ~200 PA, OBP and SLG at ~500 PA—but batting average requires **910+ PA** (more than a full season), and pitcher BABIP needs ~2,000 batted balls in play (~3 years). Statcast metrics (exit velocity, launch angle) stabilize at 16–20 games. Early-season models should weight fast-stabilizing metrics heavily: K%, BB%, contact rates, Stuff+, and Statcast batted-ball quality.

Realistic benchmarks: FiveThirtyEight predicted **57.7%** of MLB games correctly (2016–2018), FanGraphs 56.9%. The best academic models achieve 57–59.5%. But accuracy is secondary to calibration and CLV. A model that wins 47% of bets can be highly profitable if it captures plus-money underdogs consistently—Sports Insights' Best Bet picks went 3,005-3,378 (**47.1% win rate**) but produced +202.7 units of profit. Annual ROI of **2–5%** is considered excellent for a sustained MLB model. The primary validation metric is CLV against Pinnacle's de-vigged closing line: consistently beating closing by 1–2% is the hallmark of a sharp bettor and requires far fewer bets to confirm than profit/loss tracking.

## Five strategies with documented edges in MLB

**Wind-based totals betting** is the most consistently profitable simple strategy found in the research. Unders when wind blows in from center at 5+ mph have hit at 55.5% since 2005 with only one losing season in 14 years. This works because sportsbooks sometimes don't adjust totals until wind direction is confirmed on game morning, particularly at Wrigley Field where wind can shift the line by a full run. Act on weather information before lines reflect it.

**First-five-innings (F5) betting** isolates the most predictable component of MLB games—starting pitcher matchups—while eliminating bullpen variance entirely. By 2018, only 30 pitchers averaged 6+ IP per start, meaning most full-game outcomes depend heavily on unpredictable bullpen performance. F5 is particularly valuable when your edge is a dominant starter behind a questionable bullpen, or a clear pitching mismatch (ace vs. #5 starter). Sportsbooks invest less modeling effort in F5 pricing, creating structural inefficiency.

**Contrarian underdog betting with reverse line movement** has produced documented returns. Teams receiving less than 30% of moneyline bets combined with 10+ cents of reverse line movement (line moving against public action) have returned **+72.71 units at 4.2% ROI** since tracking began. More broadly, MLB underdogs win ~44% of all games but need only ~43.5% wins at +130 average odds to break even. Divisional underdogs with high totals (≥8.5) have produced +172.95 units at 2.3% ROI since 2005—divisional familiarity levels the playing field.

**Fading streaking home teams** exploits public overreaction to recent performance. Betting the visitor when the home team is on exactly a 4-game win streak has returned ~7% ROI on both moneyline and run line, occurring ~100 times per year and profitable in 8 of 10 years tested. The base rate for each game remains close to a team's true-talent level regardless of streak.

**Strikeout props** represent the largest inefficiency in MLB player prop markets. Model inputs—pitcher K%, opponent team strikeout rate, opposing lineup chase rate (O-Swing%), Z-Contact%, umpire zone, park factors—create matchup-specific projections that sportsbooks adjust slowly, especially after late lineup changes. High-K pitchers facing teams that strike out 25%+ of the time are prime over spots.

## MLB markets reward line-shopping and vig discipline above all

The single most important practical consideration for MLB betting profitability is **vig management**. MLB is a moneyline sport, and the difference between dime lines (-105/-105, ~2.4% vig) and 20-cent lines (-110/-110, ~4.5% vig) over 1,000+ annual bets represents approximately 1–2% on ROI—which for most models is the *entire edge*. Pinnacle (not available to US bettors directly, but accessible through Asian brokers like AsianConnect88) offers the industry's lowest margins and never limits winners. For US bettors, **Circa Sports** takes high limits and tolerates sharp action; **Heritage Sports** offers -108 juice to -190 and -104 on overnight lines; **BetAnySports** provides dime run lines and nickel overnight lines. Major US books (DraftKings, FanDuel, BetMGM) typically run 20-cent lines and will limit profitable bettors, though DraftKings reportedly maintains ~$100 minimum rather than total bans.

Lines follow a predictable daily timeline. Opening odds appear overnight (Bookmaker.eu often first, Heritage offers -104 overnight juice). Lines sharpen through the morning as sharp action arrives. The **largest single movement event** is lineup confirmation, typically 2–4 hours before first pitch, which can move lines 10–30+ cents. Late sharp money and weather updates cause final adjustments in the last 30–60 minutes. Always bet "listed pitcher" to maintain model integrity—if the pitcher changes, your pricing basis evaporates.

Account longevity requires deliberate camouflage. The primary trigger for limits is not winning—it's **consistently beating closing lines**. Round bets to the nearest $5 or $10, wager 70–80% of max limits rather than max, vary bet timing, browse the app between bets, and occasionally throw in small recreational-looking parlays. The first 20 bets on any new book are the most closely monitored period. Use Pinnacle, Circa, and Bookmaker as primary sharp-tolerant books; treat DraftKings, FanDuel, and BetMGM as supplementary accounts managed carefully.

For bankroll management, MLB's 162-game season with 5–8 simultaneous daily bets demands **quarter-Kelly (0.25×)** sizing to manage correlated daily exposure. Most professional bettors risk 1–2% of bankroll per bet, with total daily exposure capped at 8–12%. A 75–100 unit bankroll is standard. Even a 5% ROI bettor should expect 15–25% drawdowns during cold stretches. The massive sample size (500–2,000 bets per season) is MLB's structural advantage over NFL—you converge to true ROI far faster, making a 2% edge realized over 1,500 bets more bankroll-stable than a 4% edge over 200 NFL bets.

## Conclusion: what matters most for your MLB model

The transition from NCAAB to MLB requires rethinking your model's fundamental architecture. Your existing walk-forward backtesting, Kelly sizing, and CLV tracking infrastructure carries over directly—but the model core must shift from team-level spread prediction to **pitcher-centric win probability estimation** with moneyline pricing. Start with a simple Elo + pitcher adjustment baseline, validate against FiveThirtyEight's published probabilities as a benchmark, and add complexity only when out-of-sample metrics improve.

Three insights from this research deserve emphasis. First, model calibration matters more than accuracy—a 2023 study showed calibration-based model selection produced 70 percentage points better ROI than accuracy-based selection. Optimize your Brier score and calibration curves, not just win rate. Second, the largest edges in MLB are in **derivative markets** (F5 innings, weather-driven totals, strikeout props), not in the heavily traded moneyline. Your NCAAB infrastructure for tracking CLV should be extended to measure edge by market type independently. Third, the decorrelation principle from Hubáček et al. is foundational: the valuable signal in your model is precisely where it *disagrees* with the market. If your model merely recapitulates what Pinnacle already prices, vig guarantees losses. The edge lives in pitcher evaluation nuances, weather-park interactions, bullpen availability asymmetries, and lineup-specific platoon effects that the market prices imperfectly—especially in the hours between lineup confirmation and first pitch.
