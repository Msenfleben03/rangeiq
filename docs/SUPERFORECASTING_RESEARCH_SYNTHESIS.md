## Executive Summary

This research synthesis examines Philip Tetlock's superforecasting methodology and the Good Judgment Project (GJP) for application to prediction market trading on platforms like Polymarket and Kalshi. Key findings:

1. **Superforecasters beat prediction markets by 15-30%** through systematic cognitive debiasing, Bayesian updating, and granular probability estimation
2. **Training works**: A single 1-hour training module improved forecasting accuracy persistently for over a year
3. **Teams outperform individuals**: Collaborative forecasting with superforecasters achieves 35-72% better accuracy than solo efforts
4. **Calibration matters more than raw accuracy**: Well-calibrated 65% estimates outperform vague "likely" predictions
5. **The top predictor of success**: Commitment to belief updating and self-improvement (3x better than intelligence)

**Critical Insight for Prediction Markets:** Superforecasters anticipated events 400 days in advance as accurately as regular forecasters could see events 150 days ahead, suggesting major edge opportunities in early market identification.

## Part 1: Core Superforecasting Principles (Prioritized)

### The 10 Commandments of Superforecasting

Based on Tetlock and Gardner's empirical research, these behaviors have been "experimentally demonstrated to boost accuracy":

#### 1. **TRIAGE** (Highest Priority for Prediction Markets)

- **What:** Focus on questions in the "Goldilocks zone" of difficulty
- **Avoid:** Easy "clocklike" questions or impenetrable "cloud-like" questions
- **Prediction Market Application:** Identify markets with information asymmetry but analyzable fundamentals (e.g., niche elections, regulatory decisions)
- **Edge Identification:** Skip overly efficient major markets; avoid complete chaos events

#### 2. **FERMI-IZATION: Break Problems into Sub-Problems**

- **What:** Decompose complex questions into tractable components
- **Method:** "What would it take for the answer to be yes? What would it take for it to be no?"
- **Example:** Instead of "Will GDP grow 3%?", break into: consumer spending trends + business investment + government spending + trade balance
- **Prediction Market Edge:** Markets often misprice complex multi-factor events because participants don't decompose properly

#### 3. **BALANCE INSIDE/OUTSIDE VIEWS** (Critical for Base Rate Anchoring)

- **Outside View (Start Here):** "How often do things of this sort happen in situations of this sort?"
- **Inside View (Adjust):** Specific details of the current case
- **Anchoring Principle:** Start with base rates to avoid optimism/pessimism bias
- **Process:** Find base rate → Adjust for unique factors → Update with new information
- **Prediction Market Application:** Markets often overreact to narrative (inside view) and ignore statistical baselines

#### 4. **BAYESIAN UPDATING: Update Beliefs Incrementally**

- **What:** "Belief updating is to good forecasting as brushing and flossing are to dental hygiene"
- **How:** Many small updates, occasional big updates when warranted
- **Superforecasters:** Explicitly make Bayesian calculations, updating probabilities as new evidence emerges
- **Anti-Pattern:** Anchoring too heavily on initial estimates or overreacting to every data point

#### 5. **EXAMINE CLASHING CAUSAL FORCES**

- **What:** Identify competing factors that push probability in opposite directions
- **Method:** Steel-man both bull and bear cases
- **Prediction Market Edge:** Markets often price in dominant narrative while ignoring countervailing forces

#### 6. **GRANULAR PROBABILITY ESTIMATES** (Commandment 6)

- **Research Finding:** Forecasters using 1% increments (35%, 36%, 37%) are more accurate than those using 5% increments (35%, 40%, 45%), who beat those using 10% increments
- **Why It Works:** Forces deeper analysis and better calibration
- **Exception:** Stay in "maybe zone" (35-65%) when uncertainty is truly irreducible
- **Prediction Market Application:** Precise probability calculations expose mispricing vs rounded market prices

#### 7. **BALANCE CONFIDENCE LEVELS**

- **Anti-Overconfidence:** Don't rush to judgment
- **Anti-Underconfidence:** Don't linger too near "maybe" indefinitely
- **Superforecaster Pattern:** Move gradually from 50% as evidence accumulates
- **Prediction Market Edge:** Markets swing between euphoria and fear; calibrated estimates find value

#### 8. **LEARN FROM ERRORS (Avoid Hindsight Bias)**

- **Method:** Document reasoning BEFORE outcome known
- **Debiasing Technique:** List 2-10 ways the opposite could have happened (reduces hindsight bias)
- **Track:** What you got right, what you got wrong, WHY you got it wrong
- **Prediction Markets:** Keep a forecast journal with rationale; review closed positions objectively

#### 9. **COLLABORATIVE FORECASTING**

- **Finding:** Teams systematically outperform individual forecasters
- **Best Practice:** Perspective-taking, precision questioning, constructive confrontation
- **Prediction Market Application:** Build a network to challenge your assumptions and surface blind spots

#### 10. **DELIBERATE PRACTICE ("Error-Balancing Bicycle")**

- **What:** Systematic improvement through repeated forecasting, scoring, and reflection
- **Not Enough:** Reading training manuals
- **Required:** Deep, deliberative practice with feedback loops
- **Prediction Market Parallel:** Paper trade → Small stakes → Track Brier scores → Iterate

#### BONUS 11th COMMANDMENT: **Don't Treat Commandments as Commandments**

- Flexibility and context-awareness matter
- "It is impossible to lay down binding rules" - guidelines are the best we can do

## Part 2: Good Judgment Project Research Findings

### Tournament Performance (IARPA ACE 2011-2015)

**Competition Structure:**

- 4 years, 500 questions, 1+ million forecasts
- Several thousand volunteer forecasters
- Multiple research teams competing

**Results:**

- **GJP won both seasons decisively**
- 35-72% more accurate than any other research team
- 30% more accurate than intelligence analysts **with access to classified information**
- 15-30% more accurate than prediction markets
- IARPA ended competition early due to GJP dominance

**Superforecaster Performance:**

- Top 2% of forecasters ("superforecasters") consistently outperformed
- 260 superforecasters in final season
- **Superforecasters at 400 days out = Regular forecasters at 150 days out** (266% time advantage)

### What Distinguished Superforecasters

**Cognitive Traits:**

- Higher IQ (but not the primary factor - only 1/3 as predictive as belief updating)
- Political knowledge and domain expertise
- Actively open-minded thinking (3x more predictive than intelligence)

**Process Characteristics:**

- Update frequency: "A lot (but not too much)"
- Noise filtering: Superior ability to separate signal from noise (more important than bias reduction)
- Bayesian thinking: Explicit probabilistic calculations
- Decomposition: Break complex questions into manageable parts
- Self-criticism: Treat beliefs as hypotheses to test, not treasures to protect

**Measurable Attributes:**

- **Better overall Brier scores**
- **Better resolution** (more confident when warranted)
- **Better calibration** (if they say 70%, it happens 70% of the time)
- **AUC scores:** 96% (superforecasters) vs 84% (top team) vs 75% (all others)

### Training Effects

**Key Finding:** Training helps, and effects persist

- **Duration:** 1-hour training module
- **Persistence:** Effects lasted at least one year
- **Content:** Probabilistic reasoning, outside view (reference class forecasting), cognitive bias mitigation
- **Results:** Trained forecasters consistently beat control group by 60%+

**Most Effective Techniques:**

1. **Reference class forecasting (comparison classes)** - correlated with accuracy
2. **Cognitive debiasing** - actively open-minded thinking
3. **Probabilistic thinking** - converting vague language to numbers

**Less Effective:**

- Some other training components showed weaker correlation (not specified in research)

### Team vs Individual Performance

**Key Findings:**

- Teams systematically outperformed individual forecasters
- Prediction markets (aggregation mechanism) outperformed individual forecasters
- **Elite superforecasters approached team-level performance solo**

**Combination Methods Matter:**

- Simple averaging works, but elitist weighting is better
- **Extremizing:** Pushing aggregated predictions closer to 0 or 1 improves accuracy
- Degree of extremizing depends on forecaster diversity and sophistication

**Optimal Configuration:**

- Domain knowledge contributes substantially
- Intelligence contributes substantially
- Actively open-minded thinking contributes substantially
- **Team collaboration adds independent value** on top of individual traits

## Part 3: Measurable Performance Metrics

### 1. Brier Score (Primary Metric)

**Definition:**

```
Brier Score = (1/n) × Σ(forecast_i - outcome_i)²
```

- Range: 0 (perfect) to 2 (worst possible)
- Lower is better
- Used by Good Judgment Open (Tetlock's platform)

**Why It Matters:**

- Proper scoring rule (incentivizes honest forecasting)
- Simultaneously measures calibration and resolution
- Bounded and stable (unlike log scores which can approach infinity)
- More robust than log scores - gentler penalty for low-probability events that occur

**Decomposition:**

```
Brier Score = Calibration Error + Resolution + Uncertainty
```

- **Calibration:** Reliability (do 70% events happen 70% of the time?)
- **Resolution:** How far predictions are from baseline rates (informativeness)
- **Uncertainty:** Inherent unpredictability (not controllable)

**Standardized Brier Score:**

- GJP used this to compare forecasters
- Benchmarks against daily average of all active participants
- Superforecasters showed consistently lower (better) standardized Brier scores

### 2. Calibration Curves (Reliability Diagrams)

**What:** Plot predicted probability vs actual frequency

- Perfect calibration = diagonal line
- Above diagonal = overconfidence
- Below diagonal = underconfidence

**Superforecaster Pattern:**

- Tight clustering around perfect calibration line
- Minimal systematic bias
- Good calibration across full probability range (not just 40-60%)

**Practical Application:**

- Track your own calibration monthly
- Identify probability ranges where you're consistently off
- Adjust future estimates accordingly

### 3. Resolution and Sharpness

**Resolution:**

- How much information your forecasts contain beyond base rates
- Weather forecaster using seasonal averages = well-calibrated but low resolution
- Weather forecaster using current conditions = well-calibrated AND high resolution

**Sharpness:**

- How extreme your probability estimates are
- Higher sharpness = more 10% and 90% predictions, fewer 50% predictions
- **Danger:** High sharpness without good calibration = overconfidence

**Superforecaster Pattern:**

- Foxes (not hedgehogs) beat both on calibration AND resolution
- Make sharp predictions when warranted, stay in "maybe zone" when appropriate

### 4. Log Score vs Brier Score

**Log Score:**

```
Log Score = -Σ(actual_i × log(forecast_i))
```

- Used by Metaculus
- Penalizes overconfidence more severely than underconfidence
- Asymptotic penalty (approaches -∞ for 0% forecast that resolves YES)
- Better for detecting and punishing extreme miscalibration

**Brier Score:**

- More forgiving of small errors
- Bounded [0,2] range
- Preferred by Tetlock for Good Judgment Project

**Which to Use:**

- **Prediction Markets:** Track both; Brier for overall assessment, Log for overconfidence detection
- **Learning:** Start with Brier (more stable feedback)
- **High Stakes:** Use Log score to force conservative estimates

### 5. Over/Underconfidence Detection

**Method:** Compare confidence intervals to actual outcomes

**Overconfidence Signals:**

- Predictions cluster at extremes (10%, 90%) but outcomes are closer to base rates
- "Surprised" by outcomes more than 20-30% of the time
- Hindsight bias: "I should have known" after resolution

**Underconfidence Signals:**

- Predictions cluster around 40-60% on clearly directional questions
- Missing value by not differentiating between scenarios
- Low resolution despite good calibration

**Superforecaster Baseline:**

- Comfortable making 65% vs 70% vs 75% distinctions
- Willing to go to 85-95% when evidence warrants
- Stay in maybe-zone when uncertainty is genuinely high

## Part 4: Forecasting Process Documentation

### Superforecaster Research Process (Documented Patterns)

#### Phase 1: Initial Assessment (Outside View First)

**Step 1: Reference Class Identification**

- Find similar historical cases ("comparison classes")
- Calculate base rate: "How often does this type of event occur?"
- **Example:** "Will candidate X win?" → Base rate of incumbents winning in similar economic conditions

**Step 2: Anchor on Base Rate**

- Set initial probability = base rate
- **Why:** Protects against narrative bias and overconfidence
- **Psychological principle:** Starting number has powerful anchoring effect

**Step 3: Identify Information Sources**

- Superforecasters use **public data** (they beat classified-info analysts)
- Prioritize: High-quality polls, economic indicators, expert consensus
- **Avoid:** Pundit predictions, social media sentiment (noise)

#### Phase 2: Decomposition (Fermi-ization)

**Break Question into Components:**

Example: "Will US GDP exceed 3% growth in 2026?"

Sub-questions:

1. Consumer spending trajectory (weighted 70% of GDP)
2. Business investment trends (weighted 18% of GDP)
3. Government spending/deficit plans (weighted 17% of GDP)
4. Net exports forecast (weighted -5% of GDP)
5. Inventory adjustments

**Estimate Each Component:**

- Use separate base rates for each
- Identify leading indicators
- Chain probabilities correctly (multiply for AND, add for OR, account for correlation)

**Why This Works:**

- Prevents anchoring on gut feeling
- Exposes ignorance (which parts are truly unknown?)
- Wild guesses on small parts > wild guess on whole question

#### Phase 3: Inside View Adjustment

**Identify Unique Factors:**

- What makes THIS case different from historical baseline?
- Directional analysis: Does each factor push probability up or down?

**Clashing Causal Forces:**

- Bull case: List all factors supporting YES
- Bear case: List all factors supporting NO
- Assign rough weights to each force

**Bayesian Update:**

```
Prior = Base rate (outside view)
Evidence = Unique factors (inside view)
Posterior = Updated probability
```

**Superforecaster Pattern:**

- Small adjustments (5-15 percentage points) from base rate initially
- Larger adjustments only with strong, specific evidence
- Avoid "this time is different" thinking without quantified reasoning

#### Phase 4: Update Triggers

**What Causes Probability Revision:**

**High-Quality New Information:**

- Hard data releases (GDP, employment, earnings)
- Major policy announcements
- Structural changes in the question (rule changes, player injuries)

**Low-Quality Information (Ignore):**

- Pundit opinions without new facts
- Market volatility without fundamental change
- Social media trends

**Superforecaster Update Frequency:**

- "A lot (but not too much)"
- Pattern: Weekly for medium-term questions, daily for near-term
- Big updates rare - most are 2-5 percentage point adjustments

**Time-Decay Weighting:**

- Recent information weighted more heavily (exponential decay)
- But avoid recency bias - ask "Is this NEW information or noise?"
- Base rates don't change; adjust only for incremental info

#### Phase 5: Metacognition and Self-Monitoring

**Pre-Forecast:**

- Am I anchoring on a narrative instead of base rates?
- Have I considered alternative scenarios?
- What would change my mind?

**During Forecast:**

- Am I updating too quickly (chasing noise)?
- Am I updating too slowly (anchoring on initial estimate)?
- Is my confidence level justified by evidence quality?

**Post-Forecast (Mandatory):**

- Document reasoning BEFORE outcome
- Track: What was my probability? What was my reasoning? What information did I weigh?
- After resolution: Was I right for the right reasons or wrong reasons?

**Bias Monitoring:**

- Overconfidence: Am I consistently surprised by outcomes?
- Confirmation bias: Did I seek disconfirming evidence?
- Hindsight bias: Document what you COULDN'T have known beforehand

## Part 5: Cognitive Debiasing Strategies

### Actively Open-Minded Thinking (AOT)

**Definition:** "The tendency to evaluate arguments and evidence without undue bias from one's own prior beliefs—and with recognition of the fallibility of one's judgment" (Jonathan Baron)

**Core Practice:** Treat beliefs as hypotheses to test, not treasures to protect

**Superforecaster Characteristics:**

- Seek out diverse perspectives actively
- Update beliefs when encountering new information
- 3x better predictor of accuracy than intelligence

**Practical Applications:**

1. **Steel-Man Opposing Views:** Argue the opposite side as strongly as possible
2. **Pre-Mortem Analysis:** "Assume I'm wrong - what would the cause be?"
3. **Red Team/Blue Team:** Assign roles to argue different scenarios
4. **Prediction Journaling:** Write down reasoning to expose motivated reasoning later

### Overcoming Specific Biases

#### 1. Overconfidence Bias

**Manifestation:**

- Predictions too extreme (too many 90%+ or 10%- forecasts)
- Surprised by outcomes more often than probability suggests

**Debiasing Techniques:**

- **Training Effect:** 1-hour module on overconfidence reduced it persistently
- **Calibration Feedback:** Track actual outcomes vs predicted probabilities monthly
- **Consider-the-Opposite:** Force yourself to list reasons prediction could be wrong
- **Granularity:** Moving from 70% to 67% forces reflection on confidence level

#### 2. Hindsight Bias ("I Knew It All Along")

**Manifestation:**

- After outcome, feeling like it was more predictable than it was
- Overestimating how well you would have predicted it beforehand

**Debiasing Techniques:**

- **Foresight Framing:** Envision and explain BOTH possible outcomes before resolution
- **List Alternatives:** Generate 2-10 ways opposite outcome could have occurred
  - **Key Finding:** Listing 2 ways = easy task = reduces bias
  - **Backfire:** Listing 10 ways = hard task = increases bias (feels unlikely)
- **Document Reasoning:** Write forecast rationale before outcome known

#### 3. Confirmation Bias

**Manifestation:**

- Seeking information that supports existing view
- Discounting contradictory evidence

**Debiasing Techniques:**

- **Seek Disconfirming Evidence:** Actively search for data opposing your view
- **Update Triggers:** Pre-commit to what evidence would change your mind
- **Team Forecasting:** Have others challenge your reasoning
- **Devil's Advocate:** Assign someone (or yourself) to argue against consensus

#### 4. Anchoring Bias

**Manifestation:**

- Insufficient adjustment from initial estimate
- Overweighting first piece of information encountered

**Debiasing Techniques:**

- **Outside View First:** Anchor on base rates, not narratives
- **Multiple Anchors:** Consider several reference classes
- **Explicit Adjustment:** Quantify why you're moving from base rate
- **Team Input:** Others provide alternative starting points

#### 5. Base Rate Neglect

**Manifestation:**

- Focusing on specific case details, ignoring statistical frequencies
- "This time is different" thinking

**Debiasing Techniques:**

- **Start with Outside View:** Make base rate calculation mandatory first step
- **Reference Class Forecasting:** Find 10-20 historical comparisons
- **Quantify Uniqueness:** Force explicit reasoning for why base rate doesn't apply
- **Regression to Mean:** Default assumption that extreme cases revert to average

### Training Module Components (Proven Effective)

Based on GJP research, effective 1-hour training includes:

1. **Probabilistic Reasoning**
   - Converting vague terms ("likely") to numbers (65%)
   - Understanding proper scoring rules
   - Calibration curves and feedback

2. **Outside View / Reference Classes**
   - Finding comparison cases
   - Calculating base rates
   - Adjusting for specific factors

3. **Cognitive Bias Recognition**
   - Overconfidence
   - Confirmation bias
   - Hindsight bias
   - Anchoring

4. **Bayesian Updating**
   - Prior × Likelihood → Posterior
   - Small, frequent updates
   - Distinguishing new info from noise

**Implementation for Prediction Markets:**

- Complete training module before live trading
- Review quarterly
- Track which biases you personally struggle with most

## Part 6: Application to Prediction Markets (Polymarket/Kalshi)

### Market Efficiency Context (2026)

**Current State:**

- Daily volumes: $700M (January 2026 peak)
- Polymarket: $9B valuation
- Kalshi: $11B valuation
- "Information Finance" going mainstream

**Key Developments:**

- AI agents trading in real-time
- Institutional arbitrageurs
- Sophisticated polling (e.g., "neighbor-effect" polls used by Théo/"French Whale")
- High-frequency event trading

**Implication:** Markets are becoming MORE efficient, but inefficiencies remain in:

1. Complex multi-factor questions (low decomposition by participants)
2. Low-liquidity niche markets
3. Early price discovery periods
4. Behavioral bias exploitation (longshot bias, narrative trading)

### When to Trust Markets vs Personal Models

#### Trust the Market When

1. **High Liquidity + Sharp Money**
   - Major elections, mainstream events
   - Large volume, tight spreads
   - Institutional participation visible

2. **You Lack Information Edge**
   - No proprietary data or analysis
   - Can't improve on market consensus through research
   - Topic outside expertise area

3. **Efficient Aggregation**
   - Many informed participants
   - Low barriers to arbitrage
   - Rapid price adjustment to news

#### Trust Your Model When

1. **Information Asymmetry**
   - Specialized knowledge or data
   - Superior decomposition of complex question
   - Better polling methodology (e.g., Théo's neighbor-effect approach)

2. **Behavioral Mispricing**
   - Narrative-driven market movements (inside view bias)
   - Base rate neglect (market ignoring historical frequencies)
   - Recency bias (overreaction to latest news)
   - Longshot bias (underdogs overvalued, favorites undervalued)

3. **Early Price Discovery**
   - New markets with limited participation
   - First 24-48 hours before sharp money arrives
   - Information not yet disseminated widely

4. **Systematic Edge (Superforecasting Skills)**
   - You have documented superior calibration (Brier score tracking)
   - You consistently identify mispricing through decomposition
   - Your process includes rigorous debiasing

### Edge Identification Framework

#### Step 1: Market Analysis

**Calculate Implied Probability:**

```
If contract trades at $0.63:
Implied probability = 63%

If you need to buy at $0.63 and sell at $1.00:
Breakeven win rate = 63%
```

**Check for Arbitrage:**

```
Sum of all outcome probabilities should = 100%
If sum < 100%: Buy-all arbitrage opportunity
If sum > 100%: Overround (house edge)
```

**Example:**

```
Candidate A: $0.45 (45%)
Candidate B: $0.50 (50%)
Sum: 95% → Buy-all opportunity (pay $0.95, receive $1.00)
```

#### Step 2: Superforecasting Process

**Apply Full Methodology:**

1. Find base rate (outside view)
2. Decompose question (Fermi-ization)
3. Adjust for unique factors (inside view)
4. Calculate your probability
5. Compare to market price

**Edge Calculation:**

```
Your Probability: 72%
Market Price: 63% (implied probability)
Edge: 72% - 63% = 9 percentage points

Expected Value per $1 bet:
EV = (0.72 × $0.37) - (0.28 × $0.63) = $0.09
9% edge (positive expected value)
```

#### Step 3: Edge Validation

**Red Flags (Don't Bet):**

- Your analysis relies on publicly available info market surely knows
- You can't articulate WHY market is wrong
- Your probability is based on gut feeling, not decomposition
- You haven't considered clashing causal forces

**Green Lights (Potential Edge):**

- You have information/analysis market participants likely lack
- Market is narrative-driven (inside view) but base rates suggest otherwise
- You can decompose complex question better than average participant
- Behavioral bias is evident (longshot bias, recency bias, etc.)

**Sizing (Kelly Criterion):**

```
Kelly % = (Edge × Decimal Odds - 1) / (Decimal Odds - 1)

Example:
Your probability: 72%
Market price: $0.63 (decimal odds = 1.587)
Edge: 9 percentage points

Kelly = (0.72 × 1.587 - 1) / (1.587 - 1)
Kelly = 0.143 / 0.587 = 24.4%

Use Fractional Kelly (1/4 to 1/3):
Bet size = 24.4% × 0.25 = 6.1% of bankroll
```

### Systematic Strategies for Prediction Markets

#### 1. Inter-Market Arbitrage

- Exploit price differences across Polymarket, Kalshi, PredictIt
- Automated tools: Track spreads, execute when profitable after fees
- **Superforecasting Edge:** Identify which market is mispriced (not just different)

#### 2. Intra-Market Arbitrage

- Buy-all opportunities when sum of probabilities < 100%
- Hedging correlated outcomes
- **Example:** Presidential election + party control of Congress

#### 3. Longshot Bias Exploitation

- Behavioral finding: Traders overvalue underdogs, undervalue favorites
- **Strategy:** Fade extreme longshots, back strong favorites
- **Validation:** Compare market price to base rate calculation

#### 4. Early Market Entry

- First 24-48 hours of new market
- Information hasn't fully disseminated
- Fewer sharp participants
- **Superforecasting Edge:** Rapid decomposition while others are anchoring

#### 5. Narrative Fading

- Markets overreact to stories (inside view)
- **Strategy:** Calculate base rates, bet against narrative when divergence is large
- **Example:** "French Whale" used neighbor-effect polling to fade conventional polling narrative

#### 6. Time Decay Trading

- Markets misprice time-to-resolution
- Early positions in long-dated markets
- **Superforecaster Advantage:** 400-day foresight = regular forecaster's 150-day foresight

### Risk Management for Prediction Markets

#### Position Sizing

- **Base:** 1-3% of bankroll per position
- **With Strong Edge:** Up to 5% (1/4 Kelly)
- **Maximum:** Never exceed 10% on single market

#### Diversification

- **Across Markets:** Multiple independent questions
- **Across Time:** Varying resolution dates
- **Across Categories:** Politics, economics, sports, entertainment

#### Stop-Loss Triggers

- **New Information:** If base rate calculation was wrong, exit immediately
- **Market Learning:** If sharp money enters and price converges to your estimate, edge is gone
- **Calibration Failure:** If you're consistently wrong, reduce sizing until process improves

#### Tracking Requirements

- **Pre-Bet:** Document probability, reasoning, edge calculation
- **During:** Track price movements, note new information
- **Post-Resolution:** Calculate Brier score, identify errors

## Part 7: Performance Tracking System

### Required Metrics

#### 1. Brier Score Tracking

**Calculate After Each Resolved Forecast:**

```python
def brier_score(forecast: float, outcome: int) -> float:
    """
    forecast: Your probability (0.0 to 1.0)
    outcome: 1 if YES, 0 if NO
    """
    return (forecast - outcome) ** 2

# Example:
# You forecast 72% (0.72), outcome is YES (1)
# Brier = (0.72 - 1)^2 = 0.0784

# You forecast 63% (0.63), outcome is NO (0)
# Brier = (0.63 - 0)^2 = 0.3969
```

**Monthly Aggregate:**

- Calculate mean Brier score across all resolved forecasts
- Target: < 0.15 (superforecaster level)
- Baseline: ~0.25 (average forecaster)

#### 2. Calibration Curve

**Monthly Analysis:**

Create bins: 0-10%, 10-20%, ..., 90-100%

For each bin:

- Count forecasts in that range
- Calculate actual frequency of YES outcomes
- Plot predicted vs actual

**Perfect Calibration:** Points fall on diagonal
**Overconfidence:** Points below diagonal
**Underconfidence:** Points above diagonal

#### 3. Resolution and Sharpness

**Resolution:**

- How much do your forecasts deviate from base rates?
- Higher resolution = more informative forecasts

**Sharpness:**

- What's the distribution of your probabilities?
- Too many 50% forecasts = low sharpness
- Too many extreme forecasts = potential overconfidence

**Target:**

- High resolution (vary from base rates when justified)
- Appropriate sharpness (extreme when warranted, moderate when not)

#### 4. ROI and CLV (For Prediction Markets)

**Return on Investment:**

```python
ROI = (Total Profit) / (Total Amount Wagered)

# Track separately:
# - Overall ROI
# - ROI by market category
# - ROI by bet size
# - ROI by holding period
```

**Closing Line Value (Adapted from Sports Betting):**

```python
# Compare your entry price to resolution price
CLV = (Prob_Resolution - Prob_Entry) / Prob_Entry

# Example:
# You bought at 63% implied prob
# Market closed at 72% implied prob before resolution
# CLV = (0.72 - 0.63) / 0.63 = +14.3% CLV

# Positive CLV over 100+ bets → You're sharper than market
```

#### 5. Update Frequency and Magnitude

**Track:**

- How often do you update each forecast?
- Average magnitude of updates (percentage point change)
- What triggers updates (news, data, market movement)?

**Superforecaster Baseline:**

- Frequent small updates (2-5 percentage points)
- Rare large updates (>10 percentage points) only on major new info
- Updates driven by information, not market prices

## Part 10: Critical Warnings and Common Pitfalls

### Things That Don't Work

**1. Following Your Gut**

- Intuition without process = below-average accuracy
- Even "good instincts" lose to systematic decomposition
- Document reasoning to catch motivated reasoning

**2. Overweighting Expertise Without Process**

- Domain experts without Bayesian thinking often underperform
- Intelligence analysts with classified info lost to superforecasters with public data
- Knowledge + Process > Knowledge alone

**3. Overtrading in Prediction Markets**

- Edge evaporates quickly as markets mature
- Transaction costs and fees matter
- "Action bias" leads to negative-EV bets

**4. Ignoring Base Rates**

- "This time is different" is usually wrong
- Narrative bias is the #1 killer of forecast accuracy
- Always start with outside view, even when it feels wrong

**5. Updating on Noise**

- Markets move constantly; most movement is noise
- Update only on NEW information
- Distinguish signal from noise through Bayesian lens

**6. Anchoring on Market Prices**

- If you have no edge, you're just following the crowd
- Calculate independent probability BEFORE looking at market
- Market consensus can be systematically wrong (see: Théo/"French Whale" election bet)

**7. Overconfidence from Early Success**

- Small sample sizes mislead
- Track 100+ forecasts before trusting calibration
- Variance can produce winning streaks for bad processes

**8. Treating Forecasting as Entertainment**

- Deliberate practice requires effort and discomfort
- Fun questions often lack good data (low learning value)
- Focus on questions where you can build systematic edge

### Red Lines (Never Cross)

**1. Betting More Than You Can Afford to Lose**

- Prediction markets are +EV over time, but variance is high
- Maximum 1-5% of bankroll per position
- Never use leveraged/borrowed money

**2. Ignoring Process for Outcomes**

- Good process + bad outcome = keep process
- Bad process + good outcome = fix process
- Results-oriented thinking destroys long-term edge

**3. Abandoning Calibration Tracking**

- Without feedback, you can't improve
- Brier scores are non-negotiable
- Monthly reviews are mandatory

**4. Trading While Emotional**

- Tilting (revenge trading after losses) is fatal
- FOMO (fear of missing out) leads to low-edge bets
- Step away if you notice emotional decision-making

**5. Dismissing Team Input**

- Lone wolf forecasters underperform teams
- Actively seek disagreement
- Steel-man opposing views, don't strawman them

## Conclusion: The Superforecasting Edge in Prediction Markets

**Core Thesis:**
Superforecasting methodology provides systematic edge in prediction markets because:

1. **Most participants don't decompose** → Fermi-ization creates edge
2. **Markets often ignore base rates** → Outside view creates edge
3. **Behavioral biases persist** → Debiasing creates edge
4. **Early markets are inefficient** → Rapid analysis creates edge
5. **Complex questions stay mispriced** → Superior process creates edge

**The 80/20 of Superforecasting for Prediction Markets:**

**20% of Effort (Maximum Impact):**

1. Always start with base rates (outside view)
2. Decompose complex questions (Fermi-ization)
3. Use granular probabilities (65% not "likely")
4. Track Brier scores religiously
5. Document reasoning before betting

**80% of Refinement (Diminishing Returns but Still Valuable):**

- Bayesian updating protocols
- Bias monitoring and debiasing
- Team collaboration
- Advanced calibration techniques
- Proprietary data collection

**Sustainable Edge Requires:**

- 100+ forecasts for calibration confidence
- Monthly Brier score < 0.15 (superforecaster level)
- Positive CLV over 50+ market positions
- Documented process adherence
- Continuous learning and adaptation

**Expected Timeline to Profitability:**

- Month 1-2: Learning (expect losses or paper trading)
- Month 3-4: Break-even with small edge
- Month 5-6: Consistent profitability if process is sound
- Month 12+: Sustainable edge with specialized focus

**Final Recommendation:**
Treat prediction market trading as a skill to develop, not a lottery to win. The superforecasting methodology has been empirically validated across thousands of forecasters and millions of predictions. Applied systematically with rigorous tracking, it provides a documented path to consistent edge in markets where most participants rely on intuition, narrative, and bias.

Start with Good Judgment Open (free), build calibration over 100+ forecasts, then transition to real-money prediction markets with small positions and strict process adherence. The edge is real, but it requires discipline, practice, and humility.
